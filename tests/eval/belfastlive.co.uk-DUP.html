DUP councillor says offensive signs in Belvoir show a 'community pushed to its limits'

The signs are currently being investigated as a hate crime by police

belfastlive
  • Bookmark
  • Share
  • Comments
News
By
Connor Lynch
  • 08:44, 3 NOV 2023
  • Updated08:47, 3 NOV 2023
  • Bookmark

    • A DUP councillor has said that offensive signs erected in the Belvoir area are a "sign of a community which has been pushed to its limit".

    The signs, which are being investigated as a hate crime by police, were erected this week in the South Belfast area and called on the Housing Executive and private landlords to "stop housing illegal immigrants and excrement from other communities".

    The erection of the signs has been widely condemned by political representatives in South Belfast.

    Read more: Offensive signs erected in South Belfast condemned

    SDLP Balmoral councillor Donal Lyons removed a number of the signs after being alerted to them, with graffiti appearing in the area hours later calling him a thief.

    DUP Balmoral councillor Sarah Bunting has said that she has spoken to residents in the Belvoir area regarding the signs and says that while she condemns racism and intimidation, they show "a community which has been pushed to its limit".

    She also accused political representatives of "grandstanding" regarding the issue.

    She said: "I've taken the past couple of days to speak to residents in Belvoir about the posters that have been erected, and I've listened to the grandstanding by some elected reps around this issue, without them talking to the community, and finding out the issues that they are facing. I agree that the wording on the posters isn't appropriate but a great deal of effort has gone in to trying to avoid the actual issues being raised.

    "While I condemn any form of racism or intimidation, I think these posters are a sign of a community which has been pushed to its limit.

    "Belvoir is an incredible place to live, it's a wonderful place to raise a family, and it's a settled multicultural community where we have neighbours who have moved here from Hungary, Poland, The Caribbean, India and many other countries. These residents have been welcomed with open arms and are valued members of the community.

    "At the same time, we have a serious lack of social housing. Belvoir and Milltown have around 1800 houses, there are only just over 500 of those that are social housing, leading to families being displaced and separated, which has a knock-on effect for women who want to work but rely on family for childcare, grandparents who want to spend more time with their grandchildren, the ability for people to stay near elderly parents to care for them, a breakdown of the family unit and the sense of community in general.

    "This lack of social housing will be even worse when the high rises are demolished. There are some people who lived in those flats for all of their lives, and many more who have lived there for 20+ years. During those years Belvoir was the only blocks of flats to have security on them, making them an ideal place to house criminals who had been convicted of sexual assaults on women and children, drug dealing, theft and much more.

    "All of the community were subjected to the effects this had on the area, elderly residents terrified to go to bed at night as their neighbour had been woken the night before to one of these criminals standing over them in their bed, women afraid to walk to the shop on their own at night or worried about letting their children out to play."

    She continued: "When the housing executive decided to demolish the high rises and this community agreed to it happening, knowing there would be a reduction in the number of homes for a time, but hoping that it would be countered by the removal of somewhere to hide tenants who have been moved on from other communities due to their criminal activities.

    "People packed up their lives, their homes, their memories, and moved in the hope that their community would be protected and safe for generations to come. Then the housing executive decided to reallocate their homes to house people "temporarily", with no answer to how long temporarily would be, no answer to where the properties would come from to rehouse these "temporary" tenants, and no answer to whether the timescale for demolishing and replacing these homes would be put back again.

    "So, while I condemn any racism, any intimidation or any threats or criminality, I stand with the community, of all creeds and colours, in their desire to have a safe settled place to live and raise their families.

    "Perhaps, instead of grandstanding and trying to yet again demonise a whole community, other elected reps should speak to residents of ALL opinions rather than a select few that support their narrative.

    "I will be meeting with the Housing Executive alongside Edwin Poots MLA today (Thursday) to discuss the issues raised."

    Housing Executive Chief Executive, Grainia Long, said: We work proactively to promote inclusivity and diversity in our homes and estates and that is why it is particularly disappointing to encounter signage of this nature.

    The health and safety of our staff, contractors and, of course, all members of our communities is of paramount importance.

    For this reason, we contacted the PSNI as soon as we became aware of the signage.

    Approval for the demolition of the two tower blocks in Belvoir estate was agreed last year and clearing tower blocks is ahead of schedule.

    Every tenant who has been rehoused from the blocks has got permanent accommodation in their areas of choice and been provided with Home Loss and Disturbance grants.

    Once cleared, these blocks will be demolished, so new social housing can be built in the area.

    Our role, as the strategic housing authority, is to allocate temporary and permanent housing fairly and impartially for individuals based solely on their entitlement and need.

    We also have a statutory obligation to house people who require temporary accommodation.

    Due to the unprecedented level of demand for temporary accommodation across Northern Ireland, we have been exploring ways of expanding the number of units available for those seeking assistance.

    This issue is not unique to Northern Ireland and the pressures on local authorities across the UK, facing similar issues, has been widely reported

    We have been reaching out to local councils as well as statutory and voluntary partners to notify us if they are aware of any accommodation that is suitable for temporary accommodation.

    We have been expanding our use of private rental accommodation and we have used a small number of properties from our own stock throughout Northern Ireland to allocate to households on a temporary basis.

    As part of this - and because we have vacated the majority of flats in the two tower blocks in Belvoir - we had been considering using a very small number of two bedroomed flats for short term accommodation.

    This is to ensure the number of local households (including families with children) going into unsuitable hotel and B&Bs is kept to a minimum and this would not have an impact on the timescale for demolition.

    We have been maintaining ongoing dialogue with the local community about our plans for the area.

    We had a useful meeting with local representatives this afternoon, which enabled us to reiterate the position.
